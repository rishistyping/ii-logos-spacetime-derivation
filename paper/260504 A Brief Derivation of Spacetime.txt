A Brief Derivation of Spacetime
Emad Mostaque — May 4, 2026

The problem
------------

Why does time have a direction that space does not?

The arrow of time is one of the fundamental open problems in physics. Thermodynamics
traces it to initial conditions. Cosmology traces it to a boundary condition.
Quantum mechanics traces it to measurement. Each view adds structure beyond the
equations to break the symmetry.

This paper asks whether the observed directional structure can be read as one algebra
in two signatures. Space is positive: every squared distance is a sum of squares.
Time is also positive: t > 0, c > 0, Λ > 0. The claim is that this comes from a
single spatial structure and a sign preserved through a signature change.


Six steps from dot product to spacetime
--------------------------------------

Step 1. The metric.
Space carries a dot product

<x, y> = x1 y1 + x2 y2 + ... + xd yd,

with the Euclidean metric δij. All distances are positive, and no direction is
singled out by construction.

Step 2. Translations.
The symmetries are rotations Jab and translations Pa. On flat space, translations
commute:

Pa Pb = Pb Pa.

On a curved surface they do not. Walking in different orders around a loop leaves a
different offset. The non-commutativity is captured by

[Pa, Pb] = λ Jab.

The sign of λ classifies the maximally symmetric branch:

λ > 0: sphere,
λ = 0: flat,
λ < 0: hyperbolic.

Step 3. Finiteness.
The metric δij determines a volume form on each branch. The sphere Sd is compact
and has finite total volume. Rd and Hd are non-compact with infinite volume. The
argument presented takes the compact sphere branch as the finite spatial starting
point.

Step 4. Time.
The sphere Sd sits in ℝd+1 as x1^2+...+x d+1^2 = R^2, with isometry algebra
so(d+1). Wick rotating one coordinate x d+1 -> i x0 gives
the hyperboloid x1^2+...+xd^2−x0^2 = R^2 and algebra so(d,1). Two signatures for
the extra direction share the same spatial so(d).

The cosmological constant is

Λ = 1/2 (d−1)(d−2) λ

and in d = 4 this becomes Λ = 3λ.

Step 5. The matrix.
On each (Ki, Pi) plane, time evolution is the linear action of:

A(λ) = [[0, −λ],
        [−1, 0]].

For λ > 0 the eigenvalues are ±√λ and eigenvectors are

l+ = (−√λ, 1),  A(λ) l+ = +√λ l+
l− = (+√λ, 1),  A(λ) l− = −√λ l−.

The flow is

exp(t A) = cosh(√λ t) I + (sinh(√λ t)/√λ) A,

hyperbolic on λ > 0, parabolic on λ = 0, and rotation-like on λ < 0.

Step 6. The sign.
A flow that preserves spacetime length can rescale a vector by s ≠ 1 only for a
null (light-like) vector; otherwise length magnitude changes by s². The eigendirections
l+ and l− are algebraic null-like directions in the signature-changed picture.

One eigenvalue is positive and one negative: +√λ and −√λ. Their signs are read as
time-oriented behavior and are the source of the expansion/redshift narrative in the
paper’s interpretation layer.

The paper’s physical arrow-of-time reading is explicitly identified as interpretive,
with the boundary and claim surfaces tracking where algebra ends and interpretation
begins.
